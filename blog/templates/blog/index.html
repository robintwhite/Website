Hi!
My name is Robin and I am currently a PhD student in the Fuel Cell Research Laboratory at Simon Fraser University. My research focuses on using X-ray Computed Tomography to better understand Polymer Electrolyte Fuel Cells and improve their lifetime by studying degradation mechanisms.

I knew since a very young age that I wanted to be a scientist, and this passion has led me to be involved with quite a number of different projects. During my grade 11 year in highschool I had the opportunity to work at Atomic Energy Canada Limited, as part of a summer co-op under the supervision of Claire Simister, where I helped investigate the impact of a new fabrication method for Zirconia pellets used as neutron absorbers for advanced fuel cycles.

Following this, I completed my undergraduate studies at the University of Guelph in the first graduating class of their Nanoscience program. I was exposed to a wide variety of analysis methods during my time there, and even spent a summer learning to code in C; developing a simulation of the motility of bacteria. The idea here was to better correlate the microscopic interactions that lead to the macroscopic colony pattern formation. This then followed into my 4th year thesis project which was experimental validation. I took my new found interesting in the power of coding and wrote a Matlab script that tracks the motion of bacteria from videos taken by a light microscope. The result of this work I have up on my Github which you can find a link to in my projects page.

Although I enjoyed this work, I knew I wanted to get into alternative energy research. I completed my masters studying OLEDs and Organic Solar Cells at the University of Toronto working with X-ray Photoelectron Spectroscopy in Prof. Zheng-hong Lu's Optoelectronics Research Lab. I was the lab manager for my time there and learned a great deal about X-ray interactions with matter, as well as ultra high vacuum equipment, physical vapour deposition, and sample prep.

Continuing with my experience in X-ray, image processing, and interest in clean energy technology, I found Prof. Erik Kjeang who had just started a project with XCT of fuel cells. I can say that this has really been my favourite work so far. Currently, I am interested in the application of XCT and processing the data sets to obtain quantitative information about the sample visualized. I have gained a new interest in python coding, which I also used to build this website, and the use of machine learning to process XCT images and data sets.
